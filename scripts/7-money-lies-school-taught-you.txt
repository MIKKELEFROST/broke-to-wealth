You will spend about ninety thousand hours of your life working for money. School never gave you one of them to learn how it actually works. Thirteen years in a classroom taught you the year a war ended, the parts of a frog, a math formula you have not touched since. Not one lesson on the thing that decides whether you spend your life free or stuck. Money.

That is not an accident. That is the design.

Because school was never built to make you rich. It was built to make you useful. And the little it did teach you about money was not just incomplete. A lot of it was flat out wrong. Some of these you learned in a classroom. Others you just breathed in from the world around it. Every one of them was handed to you as plain common sense. So today I am handing you seven of those lies, one at a time, counting all the way down to the biggest one of all. The lie that quietly keeps more people broke than any other. By the end, you will see the bars of a cage most people never even notice they are standing in. Let's start.

Lie number seven. Money is the root of all evil.

You heard this one early, and you heard it from people you trusted. Here is the problem. It is a misquote. The original line says the love of money is a root of all kinds of evil. One word changed, and a whole generation grew up believing that money itself is dirty. That wanting more makes you greedy. That good people just do not chase it. So you flinch from it. You never learn it. You feel a quiet shame every single time you want more. But money is just a tool. It is a hammer. You can build a home with it, or you can hurt someone with it, and the hammer does not care. Money only magnifies who you already are. A generous person with more money becomes more generous. That shame is just the first lock on the cage. Drop it.

Lie number six. Get good grades, get a safe job, and you will be fine.

This was the whole map. Color inside the lines, raise your hand, do as you are told, and life takes care of the rest. But look at what that map actually trains you to be. An obedient employee. Someone who waits for instructions and gets graded by whoever sits above them. That is not a path to wealth. That is a path to being useful to someone who already has it. Good grades just teach you to climb the ladder faster. Nobody mentions the ladder is leaning against someone else's building. The straight-A student learns to follow the rules perfectly. The wealthy person learns to own the game those rules are played in. Good grades were never the problem. Believing the grades were the finish line is.

Lie number five. Just save your money.

This one sounds so responsible that it almost feels rude to question it. So let me show you the hole in the bucket. Every year, quietly, prices rise. The same cart of groceries costs more than it did last year. At normal inflation, prices roughly double about every twenty years. Which means the cash sitting in your account is not standing still. It is shrinking, slowly, while you sleep. Saving protects your money. It does not grow it. And nobody in that classroom ever taught you the difference between the two. Saving is a bucket with a slow leak in the bottom. The wealthy keep a small cushion for emergencies, and then they stop just saving and start planting, putting their money somewhere it can grow faster than prices rise. Saving keeps you afloat. It was never going to make you rich.

Lie number four. Your home is your biggest asset.

This might be the most expensive sentence on the whole list, because it sounds so obviously true. So here is the real definition nobody ever gave you. An asset puts money into your pocket. A liability takes money out. Now look at the home you actually live in. The mortgage, the taxes, the insurance, the roof that leaks, the boiler that dies in January. Every single month, it reaches into your pocket and it takes. By the honest definition, the home you live in is not an asset. It is a liability wearing the costume of a dream. I am not telling you to never buy one. I am telling you to stop believing it is a money machine. Real assets feed you. The house you live in eats.

Lie number three. All debt is bad.

School taught you that debt is a monster, full stop. And one kind of debt absolutely is. But lumping it all together is like saying every knife is evil because some people get cut. There is a difference between debt that buys things that lose value, a flashier car, a dinner you could not really afford, and debt that buys things that put money back in your pocket. The first kind drains you a little more every single month. The second kind is a lever. The rich do not fear debt. They aim it. So before you ever borrow a cent, ask one question. Will this put money in my pocket later, or will it only take money out? That single question separates the trap from the tool.

Lie number two. You need a degree, and the loan is always worth it.

You were handed this one as a guarantee. Borrow whatever it costs, because the degree always pays for itself in the end. And for some careers, that is genuinely true. But for a lot of people, it ends with years of debt and a job that never once needed the diploma hanging on the wall. Nobody sat you down and made you run the numbers first. Picture a graduation cap with a price tag still hanging off it, and nobody ever reads the number. So here is the reframe. A degree is an investment, not a magic ticket. And you check the return on an investment before you hand over the money, not years after. Some of the highest-paid skills in the world right now were never taught in a single lecture hall. The diploma is one road. It was never the only one.

Lie number one. A steady paycheck is security.

This is the big one. The lie hiding underneath all the others. You were taught that the safest thing you can possibly do is land one stable job and then hold on tight. But think about what a single paycheck actually is. It is one source of money. And one source is one decision away from zero. Not your decision. Theirs. The day they decide they no longer need you, your security turns into a countdown. That is not safety. That is a single rope holding up everything you own. Real security is not one big income. It is money arriving from places that do not depend on you showing up that morning. The wealthy do not chase the safest job. They build a second stream, then a third, then assets that pay them whether they work that day or not. So stop trying to make one rope thicker. Start tying more ropes.

So there they are. Seven lies, and one pattern hiding underneath all of them. School did not fail to teach you about money. It taught you exactly what it was built to teach. How to be a good employee. How to follow the map, take the safe job, save the scraps, and never once ask who drew that map in the first place. The cage was never even locked. You were just handed the wrong key and told it was the only one that existed.

But knowing the seven lies is the easy part. Because there is something far harder than unlearning what school taught you. There is a voice inside your own head that will defend these lies for free. A part of you that finds being broke strangely comfortable, and fights like mad to keep you exactly where you are. Until you understand that voice, none of this works. And that is the next story.
